# Welcome to the Inquiry

This book asks a difficult question in a deliberately testable form: **what would have to be true of a physical system for there to be something it is like to be that system in a given state?** The Cø / N* research program offers one candidate answer. It does not ask you to accept that answer in advance. It asks you to understand the proposal precisely enough to identify its risks, compare it fairly with rival theories, and design evidence that could change its status.

The target is **minimal phenomenal presence**. The word *minimal* matters. A conscious state need not include a spoken report, a button press, self-reflection, autobiographical memory, sophisticated reasoning, or the thought *I am conscious*. Those capacities may enrich a conscious episode or make it easier to study. They are not built into the minimal target.

The theory's central idea is that phenomenal presence belongs to a properly identified candidate system when four conditions jointly obtain: the system is viable in the relevant regime; its activity is irreducibly integrated; a content is causally available across functionally diverse recipients without report being required; and the content is maintained through bounded recurrent dynamics. The three network roles are called $N_1$, $N_2$, and $N_3$. Their conjunction is $N^*$. Viability is written $V$. Minimal phenomenal presence is written **Cø** in prose and $C_0$ in formulas.

That summary is only a starting point. Every phrase in it opens a scientific and philosophical problem. What counts as a system? How can integration be separated from mere coactivation? What is availability when there is no report? How much recurrence is enough, and when does stability become pathological fixation? What kind of evidence could support a phenomenal attribution without pretending that another subject's experience has been directly observed? The fourteen chapters develop those questions one by one.

**Plain language.** The book presents a proposed model and the rules for testing it. It does not present a meter that directly reads consciousness from a brain, animal, organoid, or machine.

**Do not infer.** A complete account of how to test a theory is not proof that the theory is true. Methodological clarity makes a proposal more vulnerable to evidence; it does not make favorable evidence appear by definition.

## Who this book is for

This is a graduate-level introduction written for readers in philosophy of mind, cognitive science, neuroscience, psychology, complex systems, artificial intelligence, or philosophy of science. It assumes intellectual maturity, not prior mastery of every field. Formal notation appears when it makes a commitment easier to see. Each important formula is also explained in ordinary language.

Readers with different backgrounds will notice different pressure points. Philosophers may focus on the identity claim, the explanandum, and the relation between evidence and ontology. Experimental scientists may focus on measurement validity, causal intervention, negative controls, and generalization. Engineers may focus on system boundaries, functional roles, and failure diagnosis. The theory can be evaluated only when these perspectives remain in conversation.

## The stance of this book

The proper stance is **conditional and adversarial**:

1. Reconstruct the strongest version of the proposal before criticizing it.
2. Name the target before selecting a measurement.
3. Separate what a result shows from what the theory says exists.
4. Compare versions of theories, not slogans.
5. State what observation would force revision.
6. Withhold a verdict when the evidence does not license one.

You are not expected to finish the book as an advocate for Cø / N*. You are expected to finish able to explain what it claims, why each component is included, how it differs from nearby theories, which arguments support it, where those arguments remain incomplete, and how a decisive study might be built.

# How to Use This Book

Each chapter mirrors one session in “Teaching Cø / N*: A Graduate Instructor’s Manual.” The order is cumulative: later chapters use distinctions fixed earlier. A reader may consult one chapter independently, but the exercises assume the shared vocabulary developed across the sequence.

Every chapter uses the same learning architecture.

- **Opening puzzle:** a concrete problem that reveals why the chapter is needed.
- **Chapter map:** the claims, distinctions, and skills you should be able to reconstruct.
- **Core exposition:** an accessible but exact account of the relevant model component.
- **Annotations:** short interventions that translate, qualify, warn, or direct your attention.
- **Analogies with limits:** explanatory comparisons followed by an explicit account of where each comparison fails.
- **Thought experiments:** controlled cases that isolate a commitment before empirical complications are added.
- **Rival lenses:** comparisons that expose both overlap and genuine disagreement.
- **Evidence and limits:** what current methods could establish, what bridges they need, and what remains unsettled.
- **Practice studio:** individual and collaborative work designed to produce an argument, model, prediction, or research decision.
- **Summary and key terms:** a compact reconstruction, not a substitute for the chapter.

## Reading the annotations

The annotation labels perform different jobs.

| Annotation | What it does | Question to ask yourself |
|---|---|---|
| **Plain language** | Restates a dense claim without replacing its precise form. | Can I move between the formal and ordinary-language versions? |
| **Why it matters** | Connects a distinction to explanation, evidence, or experimental design. | What error would follow if this distinction were ignored? |
| **Do not infer** | Marks a tempting conclusion that the premises do not license. | What additional bridge or evidence would be needed? |
| **Method note** | Identifies a validity, measurement, or design requirement. | How could this requirement be checked independently? |
| **Checkpoint** | Pauses the exposition for active reconstruction. | Can I explain the claim without copying the paragraph? |
| **Where the analogy breaks** | Prevents a useful comparison from silently becoming an identity claim. | Which features of the target are absent from the analogy? |

Annotations are not decorative summaries. They model the inferential discipline that the theory requires. When you write your own notes, try using the same labels. In particular, add a **Do not infer** line whenever a result could easily be overstated.

## A four-pass study method

**Pass 1 — Locate the question.** Read the opening puzzle, chapter map, and section headings. Write one sentence naming the problem the chapter is trying to solve.

**Pass 2 — Reconstruct the claim.** Read the exposition. Translate every formula into prose. Draw the relevant system, causal routes, temporal loop, or evidential workflow.

**Pass 3 — Apply pressure.** Work through the thought experiments and rival comparisons. Ask whether the model has classified the case because of evidence or because of a hidden assumption.

**Pass 4 — Produce something.** Complete at least one practice-studio task. Understanding becomes visible when you can generate a new case, prediction, objection, or experimental design.

## Working with others

The collaborative exercises use roles because group discussion becomes shallow when every participant merely announces a verdict. Typical roles include:

- **Model advocate:** states the strongest theory-relative prediction and its rationale.
- **Rival advocate:** develops a genuine alternative explanation rather than a generic doubt.
- **Validity auditor:** checks construct validity, measurement validity, confounds, and transport.
- **Boundary auditor:** asks whether the candidate system and content have been fixed independently.
- **Inference recorder:** writes the narrowest licensed conclusion and a prohibited stronger conclusion.
- **Red-team designer:** proposes a result that would force revision rather than merely lower confidence.

Roles are temporary intellectual responsibilities, not personal commitments. Rotate them. The aim is to make every group capable of arguing from more than one position.

**Method note.** A useful collaborative artifact records both the group's conclusion and the strongest dissent. Agreement without an audit trail is difficult to evaluate.

# The Model at a Glance

## 1. The target: minimal phenomenal presence

Let $S$ be a candidate system, $c$ a content, $\mathcal R$ an operating regime, and $\Delta$ an evaluation interval. The core profile conjecture can be written:

$$
H_{C_0}:\qquad
V(S;\mathcal R,\Delta)\land
\bigl(N_1(S,c)\land N_2(S,c)\land N_3(S,c)\bigr)
\Longleftrightarrow C_0(S,c;\mathcal R,\Delta).
$$

Read the double arrow as a proposed biconditional: within the declared scope, the joint profile is claimed to be necessary and sufficient for minimal phenomenal presence. This is stronger than saying the profile often accompanies consciousness. It is also more vulnerable. A well-supported conscious case that decisively lacks the profile challenges necessity; a case that decisively has the profile while independent evidence counts against presence challenges sufficiency.

The compact formula suppresses the bearer step. A final subject attribution requires a stable causally admissible referent, a recomputed inclusion-minimal positive-bearer set, and a resolved bearer relation. Chapter 3 develops those requirements; until then, read an all-pass profile as a candidate result rather than an automatic subject verdict.

**Plain language.** The proposal says that the complete, properly scoped profile and minimal phenomenal presence go together in both directions. No single component is offered as a consciousness switch.

## 2. The component roles

| Element | Short role | What it is meant to exclude |
|---|---|---|
| Candidate-referent qualification | A stable system to test | A convenient, shifting, or consciousness-selected node set |
| $V$ | Viable realization | An inert diagram, failed preparation, or out-of-domain system |
| $N_1$ | Irreducible integration or synergy | A heap of independent events or mere common drive |
| $N_2$ | Report-independent system-wide availability | Diffusion, a single output, or widespread activation without causal reach |
| $N_3$ | Bounded recurrent stability | An evanescent feedforward transient or pathological fixation |
| $C_0$ | Minimal phenomenal presence | Report, intelligence, access, or phenomenal character treated as the target itself |

The components are roles, not names for one favored laboratory metric. Each role requires an estimator family, a validity argument, an uncertainty policy, and negative controls. The theory would become circular if a measure were accepted merely because it classified familiar conscious cases as positive.

## 3. The bearer step

The formula above suppresses a difficult issue: several nested systems might satisfy the component profile. The project therefore distinguishes a **candidate referent** from a **positive bearer**. Candidate systems should be generated using consciousness-independent causal evidence. Component tests are then conducted on held-out evidence. Only after those tests is the theory-relative positive-bearer relation evaluated, normally by asking which passing candidates are inclusion-minimal and whether multiple minima are genuinely separable.

**Why it matters.** Without a bearer rule, the same episode could be attributed to a neuron, a region, a brain, and a brain-plus-environment simply because each description inherited the same favorable data.

**Do not infer.** A larger system is not automatically unconscious because a proper subsystem is the minimal positive bearer. It may contain the bearer without itself being selected as that bearer.

## 4. Evidence is not ontology

The theory's predicates concern what a system is and does. Experiments yield estimates of those predicates. A thresholded estimate can be uncertain, invalid, or out of scope. The book therefore uses four theory-relative scientific outputs:

1. **Positive:** the declared evidence licenses the positive conclusion.
2. **Negative:** the evidence licenses a decisive failure or negative conclusion.
3. **Indeterminate:** the evidence is valid and relevant but does not discriminate.
4. **Unlicensed:** the required validity, target, mapping, or scope condition is missing.

These categories prevent two common mistakes. Failure to establish a positive result is not automatically a negative result. And an impressive signal is not automatically evidence if its link to the target has not been validated.

**Checkpoint.** Invent one example of an indeterminate result and one example of an unlicensed result. Explain why the difference concerns evidential status, not simply signal strength.

## 5. The larger research program

The seven-paper constellation develops one core hypothesis through six further pressures:

- **Core identity proposal:** defines minimal phenomenal presence and the joint $V\land N^*$ profile.
- **Boundary and bearer:** specifies how candidate systems are generated and how positive bearers are selected.
- **Consciousness without report:** makes $N_2$ a causal and functionally diverse availability role rather than a behavior marker.
- **Indeterminacy as a scientific result:** makes uncertainty and licensing explicit outputs rather than residual embarrassment.
- **Ablating $N^*$:** asks whether each conjunct earns its place against leave-one-out rivals.
- **Phenomenal character:** introduces an independently testable bridge from causal-trajectory geometry to phenomenal relations.
- **Schematic legibility:** asks whether the theory supports decomposition, composition, counterfactual readout, fault localization, and evidential independence.

Later chapters show how these papers constrain one another. The constellation is strongest when its parts close loopholes in the core proposal. It is weakest where one paper's solution silently changes another paper's target. Your task is to notice both.

## Constellation checkpoint

Draw the core profile in the center of one page. Around it, place the six further pressures: bearer, report-independent availability, inferential license, component ablation, phenomenal character, and schematic legibility. Connect each pressure to the part of the core proposal it is meant to test or repair. Then compare maps with a partner. # Before Chapter 1: A Baseline Exercise

Work alone for five minutes. Write a provisional answer to each question without consulting another source.

1. What do you mean by *consciousness* in the narrowest sense relevant to science?
2. What observation would make you attribute that property to a system that cannot report?
3. What would count as a false positive for your preferred indicator?
4. What would count as a false negative?
5. Where does the candidate subject begin and end?

Then form a group of four. Assign one member to defend report, one to defend integration, one to defend recurrence, and one to audit the system boundary. Each person must propose a case that their favored criterion misclassifies. End by writing one sentence that everyone accepts and one disagreement that remains live.

Keep your original answers. Revisit them after Chapter 14. The point is not to discover whether you guessed the textbook's answer. The point is to see whether your standards became more explicit, discriminating, and revisable.
